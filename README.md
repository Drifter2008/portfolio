## My Ethos

1. What is the job you want?
    - I’m aiming for a Game Interaction / UX Designer role at a studio that builds games with thoughtful systems and clear player goals. I want to craft creative and intentional interactions, design the full flow behind each interaction, clarify the logic between different states and screens, and ultimately support the core gameplay.

2. What are the qualifications for this job?
    - A portfolio that shows previous projects as an Interaction / UX Designer and demonstrates relevant experience.
    - Proficiency with tools like Figma, Unity, Photoshop, After Effects, and so on.
    - Solid grounding in theoretical methods such as user research, task flows, wireframes, information architecture, and usability testing.
    - Understanding of game design basics, including player goals, systems, gameplay, mechanics, and onboarding.
    - Ability to collaborate with designers, programmers, and producers, and to communicate design decisions clearly.

3. What kind of verbal language would people hiring for this job expect?
    - They expect logical, clear, concrete, user-centred, and evidence-based language.

4. What kind of visual language would people hiring for this job expect?
    - Clean, readable UI layouts with strong hierarchy and consistent components.
    - Micro-interaction details, including hover, focus, and pressed states, loading, errors, and transitions.
    - Attention to accessibility (contrast, font size, focus states).
    - Interactive mockups that feel close to a released game UI, not just static screens.

5. How does your experience prepare you for this job?
    - Course projects in SIAT where I designed interaction flows and interfaces for games, websites, and apps.
    - UX intern at Lilith Games, working on game-related UX design.

6. What characteristics do you have that make you well suited for the job?
    - Player mindset: I play a lot of games across genres, so I naturally notice patterns in interaction and UX.
    - Detail-oriented: I care about states, edge cases, and how each micro-interaction feels.
    - Empathetic: I think about how players at different skill levels (from rookies to experienced players) actually experience and interact with the interface.
    - Curious and iterative: I’m comfortable reworking flows based on critique and testing, and I use feedback to find what I’m missing and which aspects of my design are still weak.
